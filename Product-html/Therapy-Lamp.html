FAQ

  1. Is excimer laser better than NB UVB?

    • Excimer laser and Narrowband UVB (NB UVB) are both effective treatments for skin conditions like psoriasis and vitiligo, but they differ in their approach and application. Excimer laser delivers a high-intensity ultraviolet light focused on small, specific areas of the skin, making it suitable for localized treatments. This type of laser can penetrate deeper into the skin and typically offers quicker results with fewer sessions. On the other hand, NB UVB involves exposing larger areas of the skin to a controlled amount of ultraviolet light, which requires more frequent sessions and might take longer to show results. NB UVB is often considered safer for treating larger areas or difficult-to-reach spots. The choice between the two treatments depends on the severity and location of the condition, with excimer laser being ideal for targeted areas and NB UVB better suited for broader coverage. It's essential to consult a dermatologist to determine the most effective treatment based on individual needs.


  2. Does UVB phototherapy work?

    • Yes, UVB phototherapy works effectively for treating various skin conditions such as psoriasis, eczema, vitiligo, and other forms of dermatitis. It involves using ultraviolet B (UVB) light to slow down the growth of skin cells, reducing inflammation and irritation. The treatment is typically administered in a controlled environment, and patients undergo regular sessions to achieve the desired results. However, it is essential to follow a healthcare provider's guidance to ensure safe and optimal use of UVB therapy.


  3. Do UVB lamps increase vitamin D?

    • Yes, UVB lamps can increase vitamin D levels in the body. UVB rays stimulate the production of vitamin D when they penetrate the skin, a process similar to the natural sunlight exposure that triggers vitamin D synthesis. However, the amount of vitamin D produced depends on factors such as the intensity of the UVB radiation, the duration of exposure, and the individual's skin type. It's important to use UVB lamps properly, as excessive exposure can cause skin damage.


  4. What are the side effects of UVB therapy?

    • UVB therapy is generally considered a safe and effective treatment for various skin conditions, but it can cause some side effects. One common side effect is skin redness, similar to sunburn, which may occur after the first few sessions. Itching is another mild side effect, often experienced shortly after treatment. Additionally, dry skin can develop, and it’s important to use moisturizers to help alleviate this condition. In some cases, sunburn symptoms may arise if the treatment is overdone, so careful monitoring is necessary. Long-term use of UVB therapy can sometimes lead to premature skin aging, such as wrinkles or age spots, due to prolonged exposure to UV light. Lastly, there is a skin cancer risk associated with excessive UV exposure, which makes following the recommended treatment guidelines essential for safety.


  5. How many times can you have UVB treatment?

    • UVB treatment can typically be done two to three times per week, depending on the patient's condition and the doctor's recommendation. The number of treatments varies based on the severity of the condition being treated, but a standard course usually lasts between 4 to 8 weeks. It’s important to follow the specific guidance of a healthcare provider to avoid potential risks and ensure the treatment’s effectiveness.
