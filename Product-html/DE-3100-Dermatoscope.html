FAQ

  1. How accurate is dermatoscope?

    • A dermatoscope is highly accurate for evaluating skin lesions, particularly for detecting skin cancers such as melanoma. However, its accuracy largely depends on the user's expertise, the quality of the device, and how well the skin lesion is illuminated and magnified. For a trained professional, the dermatoscope can offer a high degree of diagnostic accuracy, often above 90% in identifying melanoma and other skin conditions.
      In combination with other diagnostic methods and patient history, it plays a critical role in early detection. However, while it can be very accurate, it is not infallible, and a biopsy or other confirmatory tests may still be required for definitive diagnosis.
      If you're concerned about a specific lesion or need a proper diagnosis, it's essential to consult with a healthcare professional who is experienced in using dermatoscopes.


  2. What is dermatoscope used for?

    • A dermatoscope is a specialized tool used by dermatologists to examine skin lesions more closely. It is a handheld device that provides magnification and enhanced lighting to allow a more detailed view of the skin's surface and structures beneath it. The dermatoscope is particularly useful for identifying and diagnosing various skin conditions, including skin cancers like melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, and squamous cell carcinoma.
      By enabling the dermatologist to observe the color, texture, and patterns of skin lesions, the dermatoscope helps in distinguishing between benign and malignant growths. It is also used for evaluating moles, spots, and other abnormalities to determine whether they require further examination, such as a biopsy. The tool is essential in providing early detection and improving the accuracy of skin cancer diagnosis.


  3. Can you see scabies with a dermatoscope?

    • A dermatoscope can be a useful tool in identifying signs of scabies. Although it cannot directly show the mites, it helps in detecting key signs such as burrows—which appear as thin, winding grayish lines caused by mites burrowing under the skin—and excoriations from scratching. It can also reveal inflammation or crusting that may occur from secondary bacterial infections. However, to definitively diagnose scabies, a skin scraping or biopsy is typically required to identify the mites or their eggs under a microscope.


  4. What does a melanoma look like under a dermatoscope?

    • A melanoma under a dermatoscope typically appears as an irregularly shaped lesion with asymmetry, uneven color distribution, and poorly defined borders. The dermatoscope helps identify common features such as color variation, with multiple shades of brown, black, red, and sometimes white or blue. Additionally, blurry or irregular borders can be observed, unlike benign moles that usually have smooth edges. Dermatoscopic examination may also reveal atypical structures like dotted vessels, blue-white veil, globules, and streaks, which are often associated with melanoma.
