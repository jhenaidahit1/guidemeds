FAQ

  1. Can you use a portable oxygen concentrator all the time?

    • Yes, a portable oxygen concentrator can be used all the time if prescribed by a healthcare provider. These devices are designed to provide continuous oxygen therapy for individuals with chronic respiratory conditions. However, it's essential to follow the prescribed flow rate and usage guidelines. Some models, like the ReadyOâ‚‚ Pro, offer features such as pulse dose settings and extended battery life, making them suitable for daily and continuous use, whether at home or on the go. Always consult your doctor to determine if continuous use is necessary for your specific condition.


  2. What is the difference between an oxygen concentrator and a portable oxygen concentrator?

    • An oxygen concentrator and a portable oxygen concentrator (POC) serve the same purpose of providing oxygen therapy but differ in size, functionality, and usage scenarios.
      Oxygen concentrators are larger, stationary devices typically used at home or in clinical settings. They deliver high-flow, continuous oxygen and are powered by electricity, making them ideal for long-term stationary use.
      In contrast, portable oxygen concentrators are compact, lightweight, and battery-operated, designed for on-the-go mobility. POCs often provide pulse dose oxygen, which delivers oxygen only during inhalation, optimizing battery life and portability. Devices like the ReadyOâ‚‚ Pro combine efficiency, convenience, and mobility, offering solutions for both indoor and outdoor use.


  3. How to qualify for a portable oxygen concentrator?

    • Qualifying for a portable oxygen concentrator typically requires a medical prescription and a diagnosis of a condition that necessitates oxygen therapy, such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), pulmonary fibrosis, or other respiratory disorders.
      To qualify, patients usually need to undergo tests like arterial blood gas (ABG) analysis or a pulse oximetry test to determine their oxygen levels. If oxygen saturation falls below a certain threshold (often 88% during rest or exertion), a healthcare provider may prescribe oxygen therapy.
      Insurance providers often require documentation from a physician, including proof of medical necessity and test results, to cover the cost of a portable oxygen concentrator. Always consult your doctor to understand specific requirements and options for your condition.


  4. What is the difference between liquid oxygen and oxygen concentrator?

    • Liquid oxygen and oxygen concentrators are both devices used to provide supplemental oxygen, but they operate in different ways: Liquid Oxygen: Liquid oxygen (LOX) is stored in a cryogenic state, meaning the oxygen is cooled to very low temperatures to become a liquid. It is typically stored in a tank and can be delivered through a portable device. Liquid oxygen provides a high concentration of oxygen and is often used by people who need high-flow oxygen therapy. The major benefit of liquid oxygen is that it can deliver oxygen for extended periods and is portable, but it requires careful handling due to the extremely cold temperatures. Oxygen Concentrator: An oxygen concentrator, on the other hand, takes in ambient air, filters out nitrogen, and concentrates the oxygen to deliver a high level of oxygen to the user. It is typically powered by electricity and does not require a storage tank. Oxygen concentrators are more commonly used for home oxygen therapy and are often stationary or portable (though the portable versions are generally larger and heavier than liquid oxygen units). They are best for continuous low-flow oxygen therapy and are easier to handle since they donâ€™t require cooling systems. In summary, while both devices provide supplemental oxygen, liquid oxygen is more suitable for higher oxygen flow needs and longer use in a portable format, whereas an oxygen concentrator is a more convenient, electricity-powered device for continuous use.
