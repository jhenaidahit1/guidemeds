What Is A Bladder Scanner?

A bladder scanner is a medical device that checks how much urine is in a person's bladder without inserting any instruments. It uses ultrasound technology similar to the one used to check babies in the womb.

When the bladder scanner is used, a small handheld device is placed on the person's lower abdomen. It sends sound waves that bounce off the bladder and produce an image on a screen. This picture helps doctors or nurses see how much urine is in the bladder. They can then determine if the person needs to empty their bladder or if there might be any problems.

Using a bladder scanner is a non-invasive and painless way to check the amount of urine in the bladder. It helps medical professionals make better decisions about the person's care and treatment without using more invasive methods.

What Are The Benefits Of Using A Bladder Scanner

There was a time when people used to think bladder scanners were expensive and only used to check urinary incontinence. They believed they were hard to use, unreliable, and inaccurate. So, they were rarely used and just sat around gathering dust. But things have changed. Bladder scanner technology has gotten better, and we know more now. Bladder scanners have gone from expensive machines that collect dust to important tools that help people with urinary incontinence in communities everywhere.

Bladder scanners check how much urine is in a person's bladder without putting anything inside their body. They help doctors and nurses know if someone needs to empty their bladder or if there might be any problems.

Using a bladder scanner is easy and doesn't hurt. A small device is placed on the person's lower abdomen, sending sound waves to generate a picture of the bladder on a screen. This picture shows how much urine is in the bladder.

Medical professionals can make better decisions about a person's care and treatment using a bladder scanner. It helps them avoid invasive methods and gives them important information without any discomfort.

Bladder scanners help communities learn about urinary incontinence in older adults. But they do more than tell us if someone needs to use the bathroom. They give us a better understanding of the condition and help us provide better care for residents.

Bladder scanners can help in the following ways:

Improve treatment for elderly residents with urinary incontinence. Manage incontinence without needing to use catheterization. Save time and money for staff by reducing episodes of urinary incontinence.

Evaluate bathroom habits and create schedules for residents. Boost the quality of life for residents and their participation in activities.

Assist in fall management and maintaining healthy skin, reducing the risk of re-hospitalization caused by falls.

Four Things To Keep In Mind When You Buy A Bladder Scanner

If you have decided that a bladder scanner would be helpful for your community, the next important question is: "Which one should I buy?" With so many possibilities, figuring out what you need in a scanner can be overwhelming. Here are important points to consider before making a purchase.

1.Focus on Accuracy

Choose a scanner with a good accuracy rating, like ±15% + 15 mL, for the best results. Look for scanners with multiple transducers or a live scan mode, as they provide better accuracy in locating the bladder.

2. Prioritize Ease of Use

Select a scanner with a clear user interface featuring large, easy-to-read numbers and less extra information for staff to understand easily.Consider scanners with simple setup requirements to avoid confusion and save time. Some scanners may require resident setup, so choose accordingly. Opt for a scanner that requires low to no maintenance, preferably self-calibrating or not needing yearly calibration, to save time and costs.

3. Consider Durability

Choose a scanner with a durable and comfortable grip on the probe, as it is usually the most delicate and expensive part to replace. Inquire about probe-drop test results and whether the unit meets the requirements of IEC 60601-1 Section 15.3.4.1. In the long run, a probe that can tolerate drops while keeping accuracy can save you money.Check the warranty coverage, including probe manufacturing defects, and consider extended warranty packages for additional protection.

4. Follow Infection Control Practices

Following equipment maintenance guidelines, ensure the scanner can be easily cleaned and disinfected between uses. Consider wireless probe technology for scanners, especially in designated infection control rooms or wings. This allows for easier cleaning and disinfecting by only needing to clean the probe itself while keeping the unit and standing outside the room.
